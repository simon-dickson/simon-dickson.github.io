01
No Examples

ZERO-SHOT Direct instruction, nothing else

Give the model a task with no examples — no demonstrations, no context. The model relies entirely on its pre-trained knowledge. Simple, fast, and effective for well-defined tasks.

Low
Token Cost

→Start here. If output quality is insufficient, escalate to a more structured technique.
02
Magic Phrase

ZERO-SHOT COT "Let's think step by step"

A zero-shot variant that unlocks reasoning with a single phrase. Adding "Let's think step by step" causes the model to reason methodically — no examples required. Discovered by Kojima et al. (2022).

Complexity
Low
Token Cost
Low–Med
Best For
Reasoning
PromptResponse
UserIf a train travels 60 mph for 2.5 hours, how far does it go? Let's think step by step.
ModelStep 1: Speed = 60 mph. Step 2: Time = 2.5 hrs. Step 3: Distance = 60 × 2.5 = 150 miles.
Trigger phrases that work
Let's think step by stepThink carefullyReason through thisWork it out
→The easiest performance upgrade. Add "Let's think step by step" to almost any reasoning task for an instant boost.

1 Example

ONE-SHOT Single input → output demo

Provide a single example of the desired input/output before your actual request. This teaches the model your intended structure, tone, and format with minimal token overhead.

04
2–8 Examples

FEW-SHOT Multiple demonstrations in context

Provide 2–8 input/output examples before your query. The model learns your pattern, style, and edge cases — no fine-tuning required. More diverse examples = more consistent output.

Token Cost

→Use diverse, high-quality examples. Cover edge cases your real task will face. Garbage-in = garbage-out.
05
Show Your Reasoning

CHAIN-OF-THOUGHT Demonstrate the thinking process

Provide examples that include reasoning steps, not just the final answer. You teach the model how to think, not just what to output. It then mimics that reasoning on new problems. Pioneered by Wei et al. (2022).

Complexity
High
Token Cost
High
Best For
Hard reasoning
PromptResponse
ShownQ: 5 apples, ate 2, gave 1. How many? → Started with 5. Ate 2: 5−2=3. Gave 1: 3−1=2. Answer: 2.
UserQ: 10 books, sold 4, bought 3. How many?
ModelStarted with 10. Sold 4: 10−4=6. Bought 3: 6+3=9. Answer: 9.
Best used for
Math problemsLogic puzzlesMulti-step tasksDebugging
→Most powerful for complex problems. The quality of your shown reasoning directly shapes the model's output.
